🛠️ Step 4

Setting it up?
One lazy afternoon.

One 4-foot box. No tilling, no digging, no machinery. If you can fill a sandbox, you can do this.

1
Frame it
Drop a 4×4 ft wooden box (or a ring of bricks) on the grass. Done.
2
Fill it
Cardboard down, then good soil + compost on top. You don't fix your yard's dirt — you garden above it.
3
Grid it
Lay string into 16 one-foot squares. Now you never guess spacing again.
Good soil you add — roots live here
Cardboard — smothers the grass
Your yard's dirt — never touched
This is the secret nobody tells beginners: sandy, rocky, clay, dead, wrong-pH dirt — none of it matters, because your plants never grow in it. You bring in good soil and garden on top. Half the things that scare new gardeners just… vanish.
🫣 Step 5

"But what if I kill it?"

Every honest beginner thinks exactly this. Here's the relief: almost everything you're scared of, this method already deleted for you.

"My soil is sandy / clay / rocky"
→ You're not using it. The bed is filled with good soil on top — your yard's dirt never touches a root.
"The pH is probably wrong"
→ Compost-based soil settles near-perfect on its own. Nothing to test, nothing to correct.
"It has no nutrients"
→ The compost is the nutrients. You can't forget to fertilize — it's baked into the dirt.
"I'll over- or under-water it"
→ One rule, forever: finger 2 inches down. Dry = water. Damp = leave it alone.
And the real insurance? You start with one small bed. If something goes sideways, you're out a $30 weekend — not your dream. You notice, you tweak, you plant again. That's not failure. That's just gardening. Plants want to grow — your only job is to not get in the way.
🌤️ Step 6

Keeping it alive?
Almost nothing.

This is the part people don't believe. Here's an honest month:

days a month
Walk out. Pick what's ready. Eat it. That's it.
1
day a month
Whack · Feed · Plant — the three moves, on one small patch. A few hours, tops.
Weeds pop up? Ignore them. You're clearing that patch next month anyway — so pulling them now is just wasted effort. Permission to be lazy: granted.
What about rotating crops and feeding the soil? Already handled. That monthly handful of compost is your entire soil-health program — and "crop rotation" is just one habit: don't replant the same thing in the same square twice in a row, slide it one box over. No charts, no soil science.
🚀 Step 7

Want to go all the way?

The same little bed, repeated, is how you go from "fun salads" to "I barely shop anymore." It rests on one trick most people never figure out.

1 · Never harvest it all at once

Plant four beds of lettuce on the same day and you drown in lettuce for one week — then have none for a month. Plant those same four beds a few weeks apart, and you get fresh lettuce for months. There's always one bed coming ready just as another finishes.

Same crop, started at staggered times → a steady trickle instead of a flood.

2 · Now do it with the whole garden

Split your space into four long strips — each a 4-ft-wide bed you reach from the paths, holding a little mix of everything: greens, roots, beans, tomatoes, herbs. Kick each strip off a month apart. At any moment one is bursting and you're eating from it daily, the next is filling in, the next is just sprouting — and only one needs your hands that month. That's the whole secret behind "a backyard that feeds you on one workday a month."

Four mixed strips, each a 4-ft-wide bed you reach from the dashed paths — so you never step on your soil. Each started a month apart: always one feeding you, only ever one to work, then they shift forward and you restart the spent one.

⊕ Optional nerd bonus: +15% in the same space

Inside any square, offset every other row so plants nestle into the gaps instead of lining up in a grid. Same spacing, ~15% more plants — the one trick biointensive gardeners use to out-yield a plain grid.

"Wait — are the strips different seasons?" Exactly, and that's the elegant part. You never label a strip "summer." Each one just gets planted with whatever's in season the month you restart it: start one in October and it holds fall greens; restart another in April and it holds spring crops. Staggering by month automatically staggers them by season — every strip cycles through the year. And inside each strip, fast crops (radishes, lettuce) feed you first while slow ones (carrots, kale) fill in behind, so one strip gives a rolling harvest for months before its next restart.
"Can I start a new bed whenever I want?" Almost — with one honest catch. You can run your monthly restart any time the weather lets you plant something. But what goes in is set by the season: cool-season crops (lettuce, peas, roots) in fall & spring; warm-season crops (tomatoes, beans, squash) after the last frost. You're never stuck waiting on one crop's calendar — you just fill each month's slot with whatever's in season right then.
Add potatoes, beans & squash into those zones and a backyard becomes real food independence — the kind that doesn't care what the grocery store charges. Same three moves. Just repeated.
